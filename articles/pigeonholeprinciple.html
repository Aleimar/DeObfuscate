The Pigeonhole Principle

You are in a conference room together with 365 people when another person comes in late. There is this one provocative question you have to answer - is it possible to prove that there is at least one person in the room who has the same birthday as you without having to interact with every other person in the room? Luckily, the question maps to a simple concept in discrete mathematics called The Pigeonhole Principle.

             The Pigeonhole principle also known as Dirichlet’s principle (named after the German mathematician who formulated it), states that given n items and m number of containers, such that n > m, then at least one container must contain more than one item, regardless of the arrangement.

Let’s take a look at the math behind the concept:

The three cases below further describe three different situations. Note that case b can be easily seen as a contrapositive of the principle.

a. If n < m, then the function is one-to-one but not onto (not equivalent in relation on the class of sets);
b. If n = m, then the function is both one-to-one and onto (equivalent in relation on the class of sets); and
c. If n > m, then the function is onto but not one-to-one.

Therefore, if you have infinitely many pigeons in a set of many holes, then some hole(s) must have infinitely many pigeons!

The introductory question clearly belongs to the last case. To answer the question, let us consider the same scenario:

             If there are 367 people including yourself (pigeons) and only 366 possible days in a year including the leap year (pigeonholes), there must be at least one person with the same birthday as you because logically, there are more people than possible birth dates.

To further understand the concept, let’s examine more examples. As established, if kn + 1 (where k is a positive integer) pigeons are distributed among n holes then some hole(s) must contain at least k + 1 pigeons.

Example 1:
A vase contains roses of two colors: red and white. What is the minimum number of roses which must be randomly picked from the vase, without looking so that among these roses, two are of the same color?

Solution: We can randomly pick three roses from the vase. If there are no more than one rose of each color among these, then there would be no more than two roses altogether. It is clear that choosing two roses is not enough. Here the roses refer to the pigeons, and the colors (red and white) refer to the pigeonholes.

Example 2:
Find the minimum number of teachers in a room such that three of them are born in the same month.

Solution:
Number of months = 12
Substituting the given to the formula we have,
k + 1 = 3
k = 3 - 1 = 2
Answer = kn + 1 = 2*12 + 1 = 25.

Therefore, 25 must be the minimum number of teachers in a room for the probability (that there are three teachers who are born in the same month) to occur.

Here are the steps in using the Pigeonhole Principle:
              Step 1: Decide what the pigeons are.
              Step 2: Determine and set up the pigeonholes. To use the Pigeonhole principle, it is necessary to choose things such that there are fewer pigeonholes than pigeons.
              Step 3: Give a rule for assigning the pigeons to the pigeonholes.
              Step 4: Apply the Pigeonhole Principle to your setup and conclude.

Conclusion:

The Pigeonhole principle is most commonly used in the field of combinatorics and is useful in proving possibilities and solving problems. One notable application is on lossless data compression which is an important concept in Computer Science. Though the concept of the principle is intuitively clear, its applications are extremely deep. It should also be taken into consideration that the proof and how to proceed with it will be specific with what problem you are trying to solve.
